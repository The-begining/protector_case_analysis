The Problem

An underwriter receives a fleet tender.
How should they price it?

Today's Challenge

A client operates 258 vehicles across Sweden — cars, vans, trucks, trailers, tractors. They want a quote for fleet insurance.

The underwriter needs to answer:

  • What is this client's risk profile? — are they safe or risky?
  • How do they compare to our other clients?
  • What should we charge? — and on what basis?
  • Will we make money? — or lose on this account?

Our Starting Point

Assumptions & Hypotheses

Before building anything, we defined what we believe — and what we need to test with data.

Assumptions We Make

A1. Historical claims predict future risk

5 years of claims (2019–2023) is a stable enough window to estimate expected losses. We assume no structural market shifts.

A2. Indexed costs are comparable

"Incurred idx" means all claim costs are inflation-adjusted — a 2019 claim and a 2023 claim can be compared directly.

A3. Portfolio-level frequency applies to individual clients

Since claims don't link to specific vehicles, we use portfolio-wide frequency as a starting point, adjusted by category risk.

A4. Industry-standard loading factors

10% profit, 15% expenses, 5% reinsurance — standard for commercial fleet. All adjustable in the tool.

Hypotheses We Test

H1

Vehicle type is the strongest driver of risk

A heavy truck should cost more to insure than a trailer — risk is not uniform across vehicle categories.

H2

Client 18 is a better-than-average risk

If true, we should offer competitive pricing to retain them. If false, we need to price up.

H3

A profitable price exists that is also competitive

We can find a premium where Protector makes money AND the client gets fair value.

Finding 2: Flat-rate pricing is unfair

If we charge the same per vehicle regardless of type:

We overprice low-risk vehicles (trailers at 0.02×) and underprice high-risk ones (heavy trucks at 3.12×). Clients with many trailers get punished; clients with many trucks get subsidised.

Our solution: Risk-differentiated pricing — each category pays proportionally to its actual observed loss.

Finding 3: Glass damage dominates

Across the portfolio, windshield/glass claims are the most frequent type — these are minor, predictable, low-cost events. This is good news: it means the claim pattern is stable and forecastable.

Key Terms

FrequencyClaims ÷ Vehicles ÷ Years — how often?
SeverityAvg non-zero claim cost (indexed) — how expensive?
Risk FactorRelative risk vs Personbil — from actual claims data
Incurred idxInflation-adjusted claim cost — makes years comparable
Loss RatioClaims ÷ Premium — below 70% = profitable
Loading (×1.30)10% profit + 15% expenses + 5% reinsurance

Why This Model?

  • Transparent — every input is visible, every output traceable
  • Data-driven — risk factors from 2,049 real claims, not guesses
  • Industry standard — burning cost is how fleet insurance is priced globally
  • Adjustable — underwriter can override any assumption via the sidebar
Future evolution: With more granular data → GLM (Poisson frequency + Gamma severity) for statistical rigour and feature-level pricing.

Thinking Beyond

From Prototype to Production

Scaling to 30+ Clients

  • Priority dashboard — rank by risk score × renewal urgency
  • Portfolio heatmap — risk × size matrix for quick triage
  • Batch pricing — all clients in one run, CSV export
  • Anomaly alerts — flag >20% YoY claims increase
  • Credibility weighting — blend client data with portfolio for small fleets

Evolving the Pricing

  • GLM modelling — Poisson + Gamma when data permits
  • Telematics — driving behaviour, mileage, accident history
  • Claims development — triangles for IBNR reserves
  • Live vehicle registry — real-time data enrichment via API
  • Competitor benchmarking — market rate comparison

Path to Production

  • Database — PostgreSQL, audit trail, version control
  • Auth — role-based access (viewer / underwriter / admin)
  • API layer — FastAPI backend, decouple UI from logic
  • CI/CD — automated testing, Docker, monitoring
  • ML pipeline — model drift detection, retraining triggers
Core principle: This prototype proves the analytical framework. The same hypothesis-driven, transparent approach scales — only the infrastructure and data sources need to grow.
